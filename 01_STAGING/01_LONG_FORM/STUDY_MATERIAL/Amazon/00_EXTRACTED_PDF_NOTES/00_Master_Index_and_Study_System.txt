Note design principles used
1. Externalize memory
   Do not keep preparation in your head. Store concepts, rubrics, stories, and checklists in separate buckets so they can be retrieved quickly.
2. Chunk by function
   Notes are split by interview function: coding, low-level design, system design, behavioral, operating systems, networking, and review plans.
3. Use retrieval cues
   Each section uses clear labels: "What they evaluate", "What to prepare", "How to answer", "Failure modes", and "Practice prompts".
4. Separate planning from execution
   Study plans and checklists are in separate files from conceptual notes. This prevents mixing "what to learn" with "what to do today".
5. Use model stacks
   Complex interview answers should use multiple lenses: constraints, tradeoffs, failure modes, incentives, customer impact, scalability, and operational performance.
6. Reduce decision fatigue
   Use templates and decision rules. During interviews, you should not invent the process from scratch.

Core interview model

Amazon interviews evaluate a combined signal:

1. Technical correctness
   Can you solve coding/design problems accurately?
2. Technical depth
   Can you explain data structures, algorithms, complexity, systems, databases, OS, networking, and tradeoffs?
3. Production orientation
   Can you produce scalable, robust, maintainable, well-tested code and designs?
4. Customer orientation
   Do you start from the customer and the problem being solved?
5. Judgment under ambiguity
   Can you clarify vague requirements, reason with incomplete data, and make sensible tradeoffs?
6. Ownership
   Do you personally own outcomes, failures, decisions, and long-term quality?
7. Communication
   Can you think out loud, collaborate, ask clarifying questions, and explain decisions concisely?

Model stack for interview answers

Use this stack across coding, design, and behavioral answers:

1. Objective
   What are we solving?
2. Constraints
   Inputs, scale, latency, memory, availability, time, customer expectations, team/resource constraints.
3. Options
   At least two plausible approaches.
4. Tradeoffs
   Time vs space, consistency vs availability, simplicity vs extensibility, speed vs correctness, short-term delivery vs long-term maintainability.
5. Choice
   State your decision and why.
6. Execution
   Implementation, system components, or actions taken.
7. Validation
   Tests, metrics, monitoring, edge cases, results.
8. Reflection
   What you learned or would improve.

High-signal recurring advice across PDFs

- Ask clarifying questions before solving.
- Keep the customer and problem in mind.
- Write down requirements first in system design.
- Think out loud while coding or designing.
- Pick one programming language you are strongest in and use it consistently.
- Write syntactically correct code, not pseudocode.
- Produce code close to production quality.
- Use descriptive variable names.
- Write modular code.
- Check edge cases.
- Add test cases.
- State time and space complexity.
- Explain tradeoffs and possible improvements.
- Prepare to discuss technologies listed on your resume.
- Review the job description and align your examples to the role.
- Prepare examples for Amazon Leadership Principles.
- Use STAR for behavioral questions.
- Use "I" rather than "we" when explaining actions.
- Support outcomes with metrics and data.
- Avoid vague, high-level answers.
- Prepare thoughtful questions for interviewers.
- Practice whiteboarding code and designs by hand.
- In system design, cover components, storage, APIs, relationships, data flows, scaling, failures, availability, and operational performance.
- For behavioral interviews, prepare a range of recent examples rather than relying on one story.
